Part 3: My Management Principles

Create a Culture in Which It Is OK to Make Mistakes
but Unacceptable Not to Identify, Analyze, and Learn From Them

“For every mistake you learn from, you will save thousands of similar mistakes in the future.”
— Ray Dalio

Core Belief

Effective and innovative thinkers will make mistakes. Mistakes are not a sign of failure — they are a natural part of the innovation and learning process.

Pain + Reflection = Progress

Key Principles (8–19)

8–9. Mistakes Are Learning Opportunities

The more mistakes you make and honestly diagnose, the faster you evolve. Treating mistakes as bad things creates fear, blame, and stagnation.

10. Love Your Mistakes

Do not feel bad about mistakes — yours or others’. Feeling bad blocks learning. Ego turns mistakes into shame instead of growth.

“Michael Jordan enjoyed his mistakes and got the most out of them.”

11–12. Identify Weakness Patterns

  • Patterns of mistakes usually signal a weakness
  • Weaknesses are either learning gaps or ability limits
  • Learning gaps can be fixed; ability limits can be designed around

13. Don’t Worry About Looking Good

Worry about achieving goals, not protecting your image. Accurate criticism is a gift — not an attack.

14. Get Over Blame and Credit

Focus on accurate vs. inaccurate, not blame vs. praise. The past only matters as a tool for future improvement.

15–16. Don’t Depersonalize Mistakes

Learning requires connecting mistakes to specific people and decisions. Write down weaknesses — hiding them slows progress.

17–18. Reflect Deeply on Pain

Psychological pain signals a clash with reality. Calm reflection — not fight or flight — leads to insight and growth.

19. Reinforce Mistake-Based Learning

The worst mistake is hiding mistakes. Mistakes must be logged, diagnosed, and converted into improved processes.

Three Essential Questions

  • What do I want? (Goals)
  • What is true? (Reality)
  • What should be done about it? (Action)
